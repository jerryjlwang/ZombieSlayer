Advances in Neural Architecture Search

Neural Architecture Search (NAS) automates the design of neural network topologies. Early approaches used reinforcement learning to train a controller that proposes architectures evaluated on a proxy task. The resulting architectures (NASNet, ENAS) matched hand-designed models on image classification benchmarks.

Differentiable NAS (DARTS) relaxed the discrete search problem into a continuous optimization, dramatically reducing compute from thousands of GPU-days to a single workstation run. Subsequent work addressed DARTS' instability (PC-DARTS, R-DARTS) and collapse issues.

Hardware-aware NAS targets deployment constraints directly, searching for architectures that meet latency and memory budgets on specific accelerators. MobileNets, EfficientNets, and their successors all emerged from variants of this approach.

More recent work has moved toward zero-shot proxies — metrics computed at initialization that correlate with trained accuracy — to avoid expensive training-based evaluations entirely during search.
